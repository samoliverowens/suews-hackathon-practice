Starting Point

How hot each neighbourhood gets

A heat hour is one hour where average outdoor air temperature is above 35 C. The chart shows the hotter future if no neighbourhood changes are made.

How Risk Is Scored

The risk score combines heat, people and sensitivity

1. Heat

How often a neighbourhood crosses 35 C in the future weather run.

2. People exposed

How many people are likely to be in the neighbourhood during the day.

3. Sensitivity

Whether people have less protection, such as low AC access, outdoor work, deprivation, or age-related vulnerability.

The final risk score is high only when all three are high. A very hot but mostly empty neighbourhood, or a crowded but cooler one, scores lower.

Tested Options

Which changes helped?

Negative changes are good: they mean fewer heat hours. Positive changes mean the option made heat hours worse. For the reflective-surface tests, paving and buildings started at 0.20 and were tested at 0.35.

Interpretation

What this means for decisions

Cooler paving helped most

Making paved surfaces more reflective reduced heat hours in every neighbourhood we tested.

Trees need a fuller test

The tree-only surface change increased this air-temperature metric. That does not mean trees are bad; it means we did not yet model shade and water support well enough.

Water-only changes were small

Adding a little water helped slightly. Changing irrigation or soil storage alone did not change the heat-hour count.
